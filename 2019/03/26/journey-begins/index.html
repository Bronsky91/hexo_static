Waiting for Godot..

I started my undergraduate program in software development at Western Govenors University 10 months ago, 69 credits later I’m finally holding a Bachelor’s degree. To celebrate my newly found free time, I jumped into the game project I’ve been ruminating over for the past few months between classes. I downloaded the open-source game engine Godot, made my Trello board, and spun up a Digital Ocean VPS to get started!

I’ve tried my hand at game development once before a few months ago, but being enrolled in University and having a wife that was pregnant loomed too large over my head to continue. The game itself was coming along, I had a friend helping me with some of the code and an artist working on concepts. Looking back I think it was too big of a project for all of us to give up free time to create and at a few thousands lines of code later we decided to put a pin in it.

The game project I will be working on now though, will be smaller and more manageable. This is important because having a project that is “finishable” helps with confidence and motivation. To explain it briefly, this game will be a text-based chat system designed for players of tabletop RPGs.

Tabletop What-PGs?

For those of you unaware of such nerdiness, tabletop roleplaying games are where a few friends sit around a table for 4 to 6 hours playing
an imagination game set in a world full of, well.. dungeons and dragons.

Until recently, my wife and I have been playing Dungeons and Dragons together on a weekly basis with friends for close to 3 years. In that time we’ve grown a love for the game and the genre, and with a little one on the way we can’t wait to share it with him someday. Now that my son is ready to be born any day now my D&D sessions have been relegated to online only, using voice chat and a virtual tabletop.

Normally in D&D the same group of friends meet each week and continue their story from where they left off, but in these new online games
I’m a part of the Dungeon Master (person who runs the game) as changed that up a bit.

The games go as follows. Each session everyone starts at the Tavern and a quest is usually started such as,

a noble rides into town looking for adventures to recover a family heirloom.

Then at the end of the session the players (hopefully) manage to end up back in the Tavern. This makes it so we can have a total pool of say 10 players and only 4-5 need to show up per session. Even though this is all essentially playing imagination time, we are actually adults will real adult lives that prevent us for showing up online for 4-6 hours each week. This makes it great for being able to jump in one week and if you can’t make the next week the overall game doesn’t need to stop for everyone else.

The reason I’m telling you all this is to explain my mobile game idea, a virtual Tavern that lives in the canon of your weekly D&D game.
Where you play as your D&D character and roleplay on your phone in the game tavern throughout the week before the next game session. Over
the next few weeks I’m going to be building out this mobile game and writing about how I’m accomplishing it. Wish this new grad (and dad
soon) luck and see you in the next post!
